# Scoring Philosophy

## Purpose

The scoring philosophy defines how Nguyen AI should convert assessment evidence
into deterministic business meaning. The objective is not to simulate expert
judgment through opaque automation. The objective is to encode approved business
judgment in rules that can be reviewed, tested, explained, and improved.

## Core Scoring Principles

1. Scores must be deterministic for the same inputs, assessment version, and
   ruleset version.
2. Scores must be explainable at the domain, question, and rule level.
3. Scores must not be generated by unconstrained AI model output.
4. Scores must distinguish readiness, risk, and confidence.
5. Scores must support executive decisions, not merely technical grading.

## Recommended Scoring Layers

| Layer | Responsibility | Example Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Input Normalization | Convert accepted answer forms into canonical evidence values. | `security.mfaCoverage = partial` |
| Evidence Classification | Map answers to evidence signals. | `identity-control-gap` |
| Domain Scoring | Calculate readiness for each domain. | `Security Readiness = 62` |
| Risk Rules | Identify operational and business risk patterns. | `Operational Risk = Elevated` |
| Confidence Rules | Measure evidence completeness and reliability. | `Confidence = 74` |
| Recommendation Mapping | Select priority actions and service tier. | `Recommended Tier = Advisory Sprint` |
| Executive Narrative | Explain the result in business language. | Executive summary and rationale. |

## Architectural Recommendations

| Recommendation | Why It Exists | Business Value | Executive Value | Future Scalability | Explainability | Evidence Traceability |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Use rule-based scoring with versioned weights and thresholds. | Rules make scoring reproducible and reviewable. | Creates a defensible assessment method for client engagements. | Executives can understand why the organization received a score. | Weights and thresholds can evolve by version without changing the API contract. | Each score is traced to a rule ID and threshold. | Question IDs, answer values, rule IDs, and score contributions are retained. |
| Separate score calculation from narrative generation. | Narrative language should not change numeric outcomes. | Protects consistency across reports and sales conversations. | Executives can trust that narrative tone did not alter the assessment. | AI-assisted narrative can be added later behind strict constraints. | Numbers are explained by rules; prose summarizes those rules. | Narrative paragraphs cite the findings and evidence references they summarize. |
| Use risk caps for severe foundational gaps. | Certain gaps should limit readiness regardless of strengths elsewhere. | Prevents recommending advanced AI adoption when security or operations are weak. | Leaders receive safer sequencing guidance. | New cap rules can be added as the evidence library matures. | Cap rules explicitly state why the score was limited. | Cap outputs reference triggering evidence and affected domains. |
| Score confidence independently from readiness. | A high readiness estimate with weak evidence is not the same as high verified readiness. | Encourages discovery before major commitments. | Executives see how much certainty supports the recommendation. | Confidence can incorporate future evidence sources such as documents or integrations. | Confidence contributors are visible. | Missing, stale, inconsistent, or uncorroborated evidence is listed. |
| Maintain a scoring audit explanation with every snapshot. | Enterprise clients may challenge or need to validate results. | Supports governance, quality assurance, and advisory consistency. | Executives can defend decisions internally. | Audit explanations can feed reporting, analytics, and compliance review. | The audit model shows rule-by-rule contribution. | Each output includes references to evidence and rule versions. |

## Scoring Semantics

Scores should be interpreted as readiness indicators, not absolute truth.

Recommended scale:

- `0-24`: Not Ready
- `25-49`: Foundational Gaps
- `50-69`: Emerging Readiness
- `70-84`: Operationally Ready
- `85-100`: Strategically Ready

Threshold names should be business friendly and approved through governance.
They should not imply that technology adoption is automatically advisable.

## Weighting Philosophy

Weights should reflect executive decision impact:

- Security and operational reliability should carry high downside-risk weight.
- AI and automation readiness should carry opportunity weight.
- Knowledge and engineering readiness should influence execution feasibility.
- Cloud readiness should influence scale, resilience, and cost discipline.

Weights should be transparent. If a domain is weighted higher, the business
reason should be documented in the ruleset.

## Handling Missing Evidence

Missing evidence should affect confidence first. It may also affect readiness
when the missing evidence is required for a domain conclusion.

Recommended behavior:

- Missing optional evidence lowers confidence.
- Missing required evidence prevents high domain readiness.
- Missing critical control evidence may trigger risk flags.
- Missing evidence should never be silently treated as healthy evidence.

## Governance Requirements

Every scoring ruleset should have:

- Ruleset ID.
- Assessment version.
- Effective date.
- Owner.
- Approval status.
- Change history.
- Test cases.
- Retired date when superseded.

This governance model protects reproducibility and gives the platform a path to
enterprise maturity.
